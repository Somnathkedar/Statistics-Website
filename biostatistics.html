What is Biostatistics?

Biostatistics is a branch of statistics that applies statistical methods to biological, medical, and health-related data.

In simple words, biostatistics helps doctors, researchers, and health professionals make decisions using data.

Why is Biostatistics Important?

Modern medicine depends on evidence. Biostatistics provides that evidence by analyzing health data.

  • To test whether a new drug works
  • To measure disease risk
  • To evaluate treatment effectiveness
  • To plan public health policies

Biostatistics vs Statistics

Statistics is a general science of data, while biostatistics focuses specifically on biological and medical data.

The methods are similar, but the applications are different.

Types of Data in Biostatistics

  • Demographic data (age, gender)
  • Clinical data (blood pressure, cholesterol)
  • Laboratory data (blood tests)
  • Genetic data
  • Time-to-event data

Example: Patient Data

A hospital may collect the following data:

  • Age
  • Weight
  • Blood pressure
  • Diagnosis
  • Treatment outcome

Biostatistics helps analyze this data to improve patient care.

Population and Sample in Healthcare

Population includes all individuals of interest, such as all patients with diabetes in a country.

A sample is a smaller group selected from the population.

Study Designs in Biostatistics

  • Observational studies
  • Experimental studies

Observational Studies

In observational studies, researchers do not interfere. They simply observe and collect data.

  • Cross-sectional studies
  • Case-control studies
  • Cohort studies

Experimental Studies

In experimental studies, researchers apply an intervention.

The most common example is a clinical trial.

Clinical Trial Phases (Phase I to Phase IV)

Clinical trials are scientific studies conducted to test new drugs, vaccines, or medical treatments in humans.

These trials are conducted in four main phases. Each phase has a specific purpose and importance.

Phase I Clinical Trial â€“ Safety First

Phase I is the first time a new drug is tested in humans.

The main goal of Phase I is safety, not effectiveness.

Key features of Phase I:

  • Small number of participants (20â€“100)
  • Usually healthy volunteers
  • Sometimes patients (for cancer drugs)

What is studied?

  • Is the drug safe?
  • What dose is safe?
  • How does the drug behave in the body?
  • Side effects

Real-life example:

A new blood pressure drug is given in very small doses to healthy volunteers to check whether it causes dizziness, nausea, or serious side effects.

Randomization

Randomization assigns patients to treatment groups by chance.

This reduces bias and ensures fair comparison.

Outcome and Endpoint

An outcome is what we measure in a study.

An endpoint is the main result used to judge success.

Example: Reduction in blood pressure

Common Statistical Tests in Biostatistics

  • t-test
  • Chi-square test
  • ANOVA
  • Non-parametric tests

Regression in Biostatistics

Regression helps study relationships between risk factors and health outcomes.

  • Linear regression
  • Logistic regression
  • Cox regression

Example: Risk Factors for Hypertension

Biostatisticians analyze how age, weight, and lifestyle affect the risk of hypertension.

Survival Analysis

Survival analysis studies time until an event occurs, such as death or disease recurrence.

Ethics in Biostatistics

Ethical principles are essential in medical research.

  • Informed consent
  • Confidentiality
  • Patient safety

Biostatistics in Public Health

Biostatistics helps monitor disease outbreaks, vaccination effectiveness, and health programs.
